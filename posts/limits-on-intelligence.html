Sam Altman came on Tyler Cowen’s podcast earlier this month and the two spent a lot of time on monetization, chips, and AI for management. In a brief tangent, Tyler asks about an AI bubble:

COWEN: Whether or not you agree with it, what is the best this-is-not-a-bubble argument. Is it just the insatiable demand for compute?

ALTMAN: There’s a lot of arguments I’m tempted to give, but I think the intellectually most interesting one is we have no idea how much past human-level intelligence can go and what you can do with it as it does. There’s all the arguments that everyone has made. The one I would like to see people talk about much more is “How are you even supposed to think about vastly superhuman intelligence and the economic impacts of that?”

Chess - Case study where we don’t know the upper bound. In the endgame we have provably correct moves (forced mates/draws) and known combinations of pieces, with correct play that will always win. In early game, we don’t have a way to quantify openings, but we had a good idea of the landscape, which openings are possible and which are generally better. That is, an AI wouldn’t come up with a brand new opening no one had thought of. Also, the choice of opening position is generally less important than a critical mid-game position. In the middle game where things get more complex, AI outperforms, and sometimes finds the best move which a human would very very rarely play. However, for most positions, there are a handful of moves which both a grandmaster and AI would “consider” without many surprises. Extending this analogy to the real world, the end game is like statistical analysis or optimization methods we know are optimal which are already broadly deployed across society. The early game is like the structure of a democratic government or business – we know the overall approach is pretty good, and its unlikely there is a much better way to run either. The the mid game is like the act of scientific research, business decision making, or millitary strategy. In most “positions” there are a set of choices which seem reaonsable and both humans and an AI would come up with and very occasionally and AI finds a choice which humans wouldn’t have thought of. My point is that there isn’t an infinite runway for intelligence to improve – it comes down to ocasionally making a better decision. There are only so many decisions a scientist or ceo makes in the course of their work, so the impact is importnat but bounded.
